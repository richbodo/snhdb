gist: >
  This PROSPERO-registered systematic review narratively synthesizes 63 studies
  (quantitative, qualitative, and mixed-methods; N=37,553 individuals across single studies)
  on remote e-workers' well-being, organized around Van Horn et al.'s (2004) five-
  dimensional model (affective, cognitive, social, professional, psychosomatic). It finds
  substantial evidence on affective, social, and professional well-being but almost none on
  cognitive weariness/switching-off or psychosomatic health, and shows that
  social/professional isolation is a recurring negative theme that organizational support
  and individual coping strategies can mitigate. It contributes a multi-dimensional
  framework for evaluating remote-work interventions and flags where SNH-relevant evidence
  (isolation, support, boundary management) is strong versus where it is nearly absent.

limitation:
  primary: "Heterogeneity across the 63 included studies (differing definitions of remote e-working, well-being constructs, and study designs) prevented meta-analysis, so conclusions rest on narrative synthesis and mostly cross-sectional evidence that cannot establish causal direction."
  others:
    - "Self-employed workers, freelancers, and disabled employees were excluded, limiting generalizability of conclusions to organizationally-employed remote e-workers."
    - "Search was capped at studies published 1995-2017 in English, peer-reviewed outlets, so more recent or non-English evidence is not captured."
    - "Longitudinal and diary-study designs were vastly underrepresented among included studies, restricting the review's ability to identify causal mechanisms or within-person change."

  Directly informs the SNH project's evidence base on remote-work isolation: it shows

  nearly unstudied gaps the project could target with new measurement or intervention
