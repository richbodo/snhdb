gist: >
  A systematic review of 56 parent studies (48 articles, screened from 2,648 citations)
  evaluating interventions to recruit minority and vulnerable populations into health
  research. Interventions were grouped into social marketing, community outreach, health
  system recruitment, and referrals, and the review compared their relative success and
  efficiency while also grading the methodologic rigor of the underlying recruitment
  studies.
claims:
  - text: "Social marketing, health system recruitment, and referrals were each the single most successful intervention in about 35-45% of the studies in which they were attempted (44%, 40%, and 35% respectively), while community outreach was most successful in only 13% (2 of 16) of the studies that used it."
    evidence: "Aggregate comparison across studies reporting enrollment proportions by recruitment intervention (n=20 studies with proportion data; Table 3/Figure 2)"
    support_strength: "moderate"
    outcomes: ["retention"]
    predictors: ["community-engagement", "sampling-method"]
  - text: "Methodologic rigor of the included recruitment studies was generally weak: only 71% of the 56 studies used a control group, just 2 of those 40 (5%) randomized assignment of interventions, and only 21% applied formal statistical analysis to compare interventions, so only 20% had data sufficient to support their own conclusions."
    evidence: "Structured quality assessment of all 56 studies using explicit criteria (description, efficacy assessment, bias control, data reporting, external/internal validity)"
    support_strength: "moderate"
    outcomes: []
    predictors: ["sampling-method"]
  - text: "Across 11 studies directly comparing recruitment efficiency, there was no consistent evidence that social marketing was less efficient than other approaches: 5 studies found social marketing patients less likely to enroll, 3 found them more likely to enroll, and 3 found no significant difference in recruitment fraction."

limitation:
  primary: "Heterogeneity of study designs, target populations, and reporting precluded formal quantitative meta-analysis, and because most included studies lacked randomized assignment of recruitment interventions and rigorous statistical comparison, the review's conclusions about relative effectiveness rest on generally low-quality primary evidence."
  others:
    - "Sample restricted to studies that happened to report recruitment data, likely a nonrepresentative minority of all conducted trials"
    - "Few studies focused on Asian American, Native American, or other non-Black/Hispanic minority populations, limiting generalizability"
    - "Only a minority of studies reported time or cost per recruitment intervention, so efficiency/cost-effectiveness comparisons are largely unavailable"
