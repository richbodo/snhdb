gist: >
  In two similar rural, predominantly Hispanic Texas counties, the authors compared snowball
  sampling versus purposive-sampling-supplemented-by-convenience-sampling as strategies for
  recruiting hard-to-reach community stakeholders (people affected by chronic pain) into a
  research-priority-setting process. Snowball sampling recruited a higher proportion of
  Hispanic and disabled participants and produced substantially better meeting attendance
  and retention, while purposive sampling alone proved difficult to implement due to
  employer non-cooperation and had to be supplemented with convenience sampling. Despite the
  different recruitment methods, stakeholders from both communities converged on largely
  similar research priorities, suggesting the sampling method mattered more for who was
  reached and retained than for what was learned.

relevance_to_project: >
  This is a methods paper on how to successfully recruit and retain hard-to-reach,
  vulnerable populations into a research-engagement process, which directly informs how the
  SNH project might design outreach or intervention-recruitment studies for isolated or
  under-engaged remote workers and community members: it shows that leveraging existing
  social networks (snowball sampling) can produce both broader representation of the target
  population and much higher sustained participation/retention than purposive or convenience
  recruitment lacking network ties, at the cost of requiring careful attention to seed
  selection to avoid skewing the sample.
