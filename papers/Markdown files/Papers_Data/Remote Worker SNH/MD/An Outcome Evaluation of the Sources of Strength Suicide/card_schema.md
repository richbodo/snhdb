gist: >
  This study reports a group-randomized trial of Sources of Strength, a peer-leader-
  delivered suicide prevention program, across 18 high schools in Georgia, New York, and
  North Dakota. Trained peer leaders showed large gains in adaptive norms about suicide,
  connectedness to trusted adults, and school engagement, with the biggest gains among those
  starting with the weakest norms, and in larger schools trained peer leaders were four
  times as likely to refer a suicidal friend to an adult. At the full student-population
  level, the intervention also raised perceived adult support for suicidal peers and
  acceptability of help-seeking, with the largest increase among students who had a history
  of suicidal ideation, demonstrating that a socioecological, peer-led messaging
  intervention can shift schoolwide norms linked to suicide risk rather than relying solely
  on individual screening or referral.

  primary: "All measures were self-report and, for the general student population, surveys were anonymous and unlinked across time points, so individual-level pre-post change could not be assessed and only school-aggregate scores were compared."
  others:

    - "Program developer Mark LoMurray is president/owner of Sources of Strength, Inc., a financial conflict of interest disclosed by the authors."
    - "Referral effects were significant only in a subset of larger schools, and the study could not determine why rural/smaller-school peer leaders showed no significant referral increase."

  Provides strong causal (cluster-RCT) evidence that a peer-led, socioecological messaging

  among trained leaders -- directly relevant to designing peer-support and norm-shifting
  interventions for isolation/suicide-risk in the SNH project, and to the evidence question
