gist: >
  A systematic review with narrative synthesis (no meta-analysis) of 116 papers from 115
  studies, plus 31 prior literature reviews, on barriers and strategies for recruiting and
  retaining socially, culturally, or financially disadvantaged 'hard-to-reach' groups in
  health research. It organizes strategies across five stages -- sampling,
  recruitment/consent, data collection, intervention delivery, and retention/attrition --
  and finds the evidence base is dominated by descriptive and qualitative studies (only 9
  RCTs), with community partnerships, non-probability sampling, multi-channel contact
  tracking, and incentives repeatedly identified as effective but resource-intensive
  strategies. It concludes that reaching disadvantaged/marginalized populations requires
  researchers to budget for extended timeframes and higher costs and to build community
  partnerships rather than relying on standard probability sampling.

limitation:
  primary: "The authors' own stated main limitation is extensive methodological heterogeneity across the 116 included studies, which prohibited statistical pooling (no meta-analysis) and, combined with the predominance of low-level descriptive/case-study evidence, prevents conclusive claims about which recruitment/retention strategies are actually effective."

relevance_to_project: >
  The SNH project's remote-worker and open-source populations of interest -- socially
  isolated, disengaged, or burned-out individuals -- are themselves a 'hard-to-reach' group
  in the sense this review defines: people less likely to volunteer for surveys, interviews,
  or intervention studies. This paper is a methods reference for how the project should
  design recruitment and retention for its own research and outreach: favor community-
  partnership and non-probability sampling over random sampling, budget extra time/cost, use
  multi-channel persistent contact tracking, and consider incentives -- while being honest
  that most of this evidence is descriptive rather than RCT-tested.
