# SNHDB Corpus Evaluation — 2026-07-07

Full-corpus audit of PDFs, markdown conversions, and metadata cards. All numbers
were produced by the re-runnable harness in `tools/audit/` (see "Methodology"
at the bottom); machine-readable detail is in `tools/audit/out/*.json`.

## Headline

**The markdown conversions are in good shape. The cards are not.** Paper bodies
are essentially complete (median markdown/PDF-text ratio 1.08; only one PDF was
page-truncated). But the metadata cards — the search index for the whole corpus —
were generated by a templated automated pass: 100% of gists are boilerplate,
~37% of the DOIs present are wrong or dead, half the cards lack a DOI entirely,
and a quarter have empty or garbage author lists.

## 1. Conversion quality (markdown bodies) — GOOD, with a short punch list

For the 301 conversions matched to a PDF with extractable text, the ratio of
markdown characters to raw `pdftotext` characters is: min 0.58, 10th percentile
0.96, median 1.08. The Marker converter's per-page metadata (`page_stats`)
matches the PDF page count in all but one case. Conclusion: **systematic body
truncation did not happen.**

Specific problems found:

- **1 page-truncated conversion**: `World Health Statistics 2023` — converter
  processed 50 of 136 pages (likely a converter page cap).
- **2 near-empty conversions** (<1 KB): `Open Source Articles Research
  Spreadsheet` (372 chars) and `Open Source and Mental Health Reddit Discussion
  Rust Community` (412 chars). Sources are a spreadsheet and a web discussion;
  the conversions carry almost no content.
- **1 corrupt PDF**: `papers/Remote Worker SNH/UyBico et al 2007 - Recruiting
  Vulnerable Populations into Research.pdf` — poppler cannot parse it ("May not
  be a PDF file"). Needs re-download. It also has no conversion.
- **5 unconverted PDFs**, of which 2 are `.pdf.pdf` double-extension duplicates
  (Dingel & Neiman 2020; Podsakoff 2003 "(Duplicate)"), 2 are PDF renderings of
  .docx files whose .docx *was* converted, and 1 is the corrupt UyBico PDF.
  So only UyBico represents genuinely missing content.
- **170 conversions end without sentence-final punctuation**, but spot-checking
  shows these are almost all benign journal back-matter (received/accepted
  dates, license URLs, author emails), not truncation. Ratio + page evidence
  above confirms this. Not worth repairing per-file.
- Some conversions dropped the paper's journal/DOI header line (already noted
  in CLAUDE.md); this matters only because the card generator then had no
  reliable DOI to read — see §3.

## 2. Corpus structure and file hygiene — MESSY

- **Duplicate PDFs**: Schaufeli 2004 (JOB) exists in both `Academic/01
  Academic - Extended/` and `Remote Workers/01 Extended - Remote Workers/`;
  "The Effects of Remote Work on Collaboration Among Information Workers"
  exists twice in Remote Workers. Plus `(1)`/`(2)`/`(Duplicate)` suffix copies
  and duplicate conversion folders (e.g. two `Does Telework Stress Employees
  Out` conversions, both referenced by two near-identical cards).
- **2 stray PDFs sitting inside the MD tree**
  (`.../01 Extended - Remote Workers/MD/*.pdf`).
- **Folder names with trailing spaces / unicode dashes** break card
  `source.markdown` links (e.g. the Wyman 2019 card points at a folder whose
  name ends in a space).
- **Combined card files vs individual card files are out of sync**: 8
  conversions have a local `card_schema.md` but no entry in the combined
  `*_Card_Schemas.md` (which is what the search protocol greps), and 26 titles
  appear on more than one card. There is no declared source of truth.
- Each combined card file ends with a stray non-YAML footer line
  (`Source Word file: ...`) — cosmetic, but it breaks strict YAML parsing of
  the last document.

## 3. DOIs — BAD (worst finding, fully quantified)

Of **319 cards**: 167 have a `doi:`, 152 have none.

Every card DOI was resolved against Crossref and the registered title was
compared with the card title *and* the source folder name
(`tools/audit/out/crossref.json`, verdicts in `doi_verdicts.json`):

| Verdict | Count | % of cards with DOI |
|---|---|---|
| Correct (title similarity ≥ 0.8) | 98 | 59% |
| Borderline (0.6–0.8, needs eyeball) | 7 | 4% |
| **Resolves to a different paper** | **47** | **28%** |
| **Does not resolve (404)** | **15** | **9%** |

Failure modes are recognizable:

- *Reference-list capture*: the generator grabbed a DOI cited **by** the paper.
  E.g. the card for "Resilience during uncertainty? Greater social
  connectedness during COVID-19..." carries `10.1176/appi.ajp.160.2.221`, which
  is "Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: A Review" (2003).
- *Truncated/typo DOIs*: `10.1038/mp`, `10.1016/j.drugalcdep.2008`,
  `10.1016/i.chb.2017.12.010` (`i` for `j`), `10.1097/jom.00000000000002097`
  (extra zeros), suffixes like `/full` copied from URLs.
- Note: all 167 card DOIs do appear *somewhere* in their source text — i.e.
  the generator extracted real strings, just often the wrong ones.

For the 152 cards without a DOI, a Crossref bibliographic search on the source
name found a **high-confidence DOI (title similarity ≥ 0.75) for 77** of them.
Most of the remainder are blog posts / industry articles that legitimately have
no DOI, but they are not marked as such — `doi: null` currently means both
"has no DOI" and "nobody looked".

## 4. Card content — SYSTEMATICALLY TEMPLATED

The cards were clearly produced by a mechanical extraction pass, not by reading
the papers:

- **Gist: 319/319 (100%)** begin with the same template: *"This source gives
  the project a retrieval card for …"* followed by a sentence copied from the
  paper. The gist field carries no summarization.
- **`relevance_to_project`: 319/319** are one of just **5** topic-level
  boilerplate strings (one per topic folder, 149/60/51/44/15).
- **Claims** are 1–3 sentences lifted verbatim (often the aims statement, not
  findings), with identical `outcomes:`/`predictors:` tag lists pasted on every
  claim of a card — the controlled-vocabulary tags the search protocol relies
  on are largely noise.
- **77 cards (24%) have empty or garbage `authors:`** — e.g.
  `["Nutrition", "Terr, W th", "Westwood"]` for Hidaka's depression-and-
  modernity review, `["Berkeley", "California"]` for "Job Burnout".
- **`venue.name` is frequently wrong** (at least 10 flagrant cases contain the
  author byline, a URL, or the paper's own title), and `citation:` strings
  built from these fields are therefore wrong too.
- **151 cards (47%) have `title:` equal to the filename**, not the paper title.
- **167 cards use a raw DOI as `id:`** instead of the slug convention required
  by `card-schema.yaml` — and since ~37% of DOIs are wrong, many ids are wrong
  identifiers on top of being the wrong format.
- **Population blocks**: 77 are explicit placeholders ("mental-health study
  participants or policy target population", "details not clearly
  extractable"); `sector:` lists are copy-pasted
  (`["tech","open-source","education","healthcare",…]` on a WHO statistics
  report and a depression review alike).
- 8 cards missing `year`. 26 duplicate titles across cards.

**Practical impact on search**: Stage-1 search (grep the combined card files)
currently matches against templated gists, noisy tags, and filename-titles.
Recall is propped up by the verbatim claim sentences; precision and the
trustworthiness of citations (DOI/author/venue) are poor. Every downstream
citation produced from card metadata alone is suspect — consistent with the
pitfalls already listed in CLAUDE.md, now quantified.

## Methodology / how to re-run

```
python3 tools/audit/build_manifest.py   # inventories PDFs/conversions/cards (pdfinfo, pdftotext; ~4 min)
python3 tools/audit/analyze.py          # emits out/issues.json + count summary
python3 tools/audit/crossref_check.py   # verifies every card DOI against Crossref (~6 min, 1 req/s)
```

Requires: Homebrew `poppler` (pdftotext/pdfinfo), `qpdf` (installed 2026-07-07),
Python `pyyaml`. Outputs land in `tools/audit/out/` (git-ignorable).

Caveats: name-based PDF↔conversion matching has a small number of artifacts
(unicode dashes, `(1)` suffixes); "wrong-paper" DOI verdicts use title
similarity < 0.6 against both card title and folder name — the 7 borderline
cases need a human eye.
